TWS

We both were awake before dawn and before the sun struck any cliffs within sight. A whole night through of stars. We didn’t even put the tarp up.  A hearty breakfast of cream of wheat and apples, java, tang.

The route up through the Redwall waited for us, and we began the climb. With the energy of morning we climbed all the way up to the base of the Temple Butte, with hardly a stop for breath. A traverse across from a ramp delivered us to the gully leading up, and a tall cairn.  Reassuring.  A scramble up 100ft. or so, and we had to drop packs for a short pitch that led up to the main ramp. Jim climbed it, set up his belay, and jerked, cursed, sweated both packs up from below. All I could do was yell advice that, instead of helping, ended up confusing.

 Once we made it up there with both packs and ourselves intact, the rest of the climb was mostly a “walk up”.  But what a route! The final pitch we were able to climb packs on. It led out to the very end of the limestone spire, on the end of the backbone of wall jutting out from the side of Flint Canyon. The fault cut right through it.

As cracks are routes for climbers of El Cap and Half Dome, faults in the Canyon provide the most straightforward route through immense, rugged, hard country.  They are old patterns that surface upon the new. The faults are old breaks in the rock below, and though these mountains are long gone, the forces that bore them are still here. Our route from upper Tapeats to here and beyond are led by the fault.

Flint Saddle. It is a double saddle, or two seater. There’s a big hunk of Supai right in the middle, with a saddle on either side. We came over the saddle on the west side – naturally, since we approached from that side. The view down into Tuna began at our toes, and extended downward and outward, miles and miles out to its mouth at the Colorado -- somewhere down there unseen, and to the South Rim, not far from home.

 JRS

 Tuna Creek. It was a beautiful day. The route splendid and conditions perfect. Up through the Redwall the fault route was almost more of a walk than a climb. We roped the packs up once, and almost didn’t need to then. The last step was a scramble up and around into the sunlight on top of a bare rock spine;  the top of the Redwall. It was very beautiful.

Then came the tedious traverse of the Supai to the saddle. We ate lunch in the sun there in the saddle, looking down into a familiar canyon. Just a few months ago we looked across at the same slope through the telescope at Hermit’s Rest.

It was an interesting descent also, not quite the routine jaunt I imagined it would be. There were barrier falls in the Redwall that had subtle solutions. One of them involving a beautiful example of a fault crack. The fault structure of this hike keeps reoccurring.

We are camped now at the beginning of the Tapeats in the northern arm of Tuna 1/4 mile from the fork. There is a mescal pit nearby.
