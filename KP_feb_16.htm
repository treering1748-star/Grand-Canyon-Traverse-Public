JRS

 Unsuccessful day.  We were hoping that one of the arms of Deer Creek northeast of Vaughn Spring would contain a route onto the Esplanade.  Butchart said there was a route in the northeast arm, but was ambiguous about which one.  Apparently he meant the other because it sure wasn’t the one we tried.  About 1/4 mile from the fork we were stopped by a deep plunge pool in the Redwall.  There was a crack to the left, about a 30ft. pitch.  Both Tom and I tried it but could only get up halfway.  We could have managed it under other circumstances, I believe, but there was too much hanging in the balance.  Even a sprained ankle this deep in the canyon would be a disaster.  We also had no assurance that the route would go any further past the bottom two pitches.  I must admit I was scared when I made my attempt.  It was beyond me this day.  We turned our backs on it. 

We spent a couple hours fishing in the afternoon, and our luck held true.  No fish.  The river is up about 4ft. from yesterday and the creek mouth is a totally different place.  I don’t know if that’s why they weren’t biting, or if other factors are at play.  Fish are capricious creatures.  Perhaps the gods are angry with us. 

We went through our food bags and came up with about four days.  Not enough, yet close enough to being adequate that we agonized a long time about spending another day’s time and effort tomorrow attempting to recover the cache by the trail.  And that, I’m afraid, is what we’re going to do.  This shouldn’t throw us seriously off schedule, but it will be a considerable effort, no laughing matter.  It has cast us into a slump and we need a success to pull us out of it. 

Tomorrow we’ll break camp and move up into Surprise Valley.  We’ll empty our packs and hike, about 12 miles one way, to the cache and back all in one day.  It makes me tired thinking about it. 

The weather remains gray, though the overcast is thinner and there have been some brief breaks.  The winds and clouds were coming out of the west yesterday evening when a clearing trend was noted, Orion appearing for a time in his entirety.  But now they are out of the southwest again and merely seem to tease us with faint and ephemeral patches of blue here and there.  Some sunlight would certainly do wonders for our morale. 

We caught a view of Great Thumb today from the upper canyon.  We could see Great Thumb and Tahuta Points, lightly snow covered.  Could also see Powell Plateau from the river, no longer misty, but apparently deep in snow.  Looked cold. 

We reconnoitered this evening looking for a trail or route to Surprise Valley.  We did not find so much as a trace, though we traversed the entire east side of the valley as far as the spring before it got too dark to proceed.  The route, we feel, must go up the canyon above Deer Spring.  Unfortunately we did not get far enough to ascertain this.  We should have taken the time to do this yesterday.  But what good is hindsight to us now?  In any event were going to head up and out that way tomorrow and if it doesn’t work will try a possible route Tommy spotted along a ledge traversing Cogswell Butte. 

This has been a day of great uncertainties.  Still nothing is resolved.  All we can do is persevere and proceed. 

TWS

This morning found us headed up Deer Creek -- once more boulder hopping.  The great red walls closed in on both sides up at Vaughn Spring and our planned route up the left NE fork came to an abrupt halt.  The creek bed turned into chute winding through the limestone.  Typical Redwall pattern -- winding narrow channels with wall to wall pools – deep pools with boulder chalk stones above.  Impassable.  Or as much Butchart says, “impossible without hardware”.

We tried though.  A narrow crack through the limestone led up and onto a huge Redwall slab leaning away from the left hand wall.  It looked like it may get us up and over this section.  The uncertainty of it all shook my resolve and I had no will to climb.  When I was all roped up and ready I couldn’t even begin the pitch.  It’s a tough little climb, something Jim and I know we could do, but it wasn’t worth the risk -- to us and the whole trip.  We just weren’t sure if this was the route Butchart described (ambiguously) or not, or if there were greater or more obstacles ahead.

Now are planning for a long day tomorrow to retrieve the cache and return to Surprise Valley.  Our dwindling food supplies would make it imperative to reach the Bass cache without delay.  Another risk we won’t take when food waits within reach from here.

Our setback today and in being unable to make it to the cache lent a sobering effect on us.  Worry and concern.  Homesickness and missing our babies and ladies always brings on the question faced by all adventurers and explorers I’m sure: “What am I doing here?”  It seems that the most obvious thing and most important is -- just to make it.  To complete the trip, to survive, to meet the challenge.
