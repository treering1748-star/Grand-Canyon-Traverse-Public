Grand Canyon Traverse

 

A Perspective, 25 Years Hence

 Tom Swetnam, March 2005

     In the late winters of 1978 and 1980, my brother Jim and I traversed long sections of the Grand Canyon on foot.  For us, these trips were more than just hikes.  They were intentional exposures of our lives and our senses to powerful doses of beauty, pain, excitement, exhaustion, inspiration, fear, exaltation, misery, and ultimately, exultation. 

     These two 18-day journeys were the most difficult physical challenges I have endured, and the most intense wilderness experiences of my life.  Over the past 25 years I have drawn upon these Canyon experiences -- for confidence that I can endure great hardship, and for a serenity that comes from knowledge that such awesome places exist on this planet, that I visited them with considerable effort and risk to my life, and that I survived to savor and share the memories! 

     I refer to the hikes here as “traverses” because they were length-wise routes in down (1978) and up river (1980) directions within the Canyon.  To travel by foot down or up river in the Canyon, while staying entirely below the rim, requires that one traverse across the many side canyons that feed into the main canyon of the Colorado River. 

     The Canyon is, of course, an incredibly rugged terrain.  In addition to the main gorge containing the Colorado, there are hundreds of branching side canyons, with side, side canyons, and side, side, side canyons (etc.).  Many of the side canyons are deeper than any canyon east of the Mississippi.  Indeed, some are deeper than most canyons in the western hemisphere.  Hence, traveling lengthwise in the Canyon requires that a hiker (1) traverse laboriously along benches and talus slopes around the heads of each side, side, side, etc. canyon; (2) travel down the main side canyons to the river and then traverse along the river beaches or talus slopes above the river,  then back up another side canyon to come around its head; or (3) traverse directly across the side canyons, in a perpendicular fashion, via “fault ravines”, which create passable routes through cliff barriers such as the Redwall Formation.  Usually, in any given section of the Canyon, only one or two of these traversing strategies are possible, so we used a combination of all three.   

     No matter what the strategy followed, off-trail hiking in the Canyon is among the most difficult and challenging to be found anywhere.  First, there is the tremendous topography.  There are cliffs and angle-of-repose slopes that are hundreds to thousands of feet high in almost all directions.  It simply is impossible to go to most off-trail places in the canyon, unless you are prepared to climb or scramble up, down, around, over or across cliffs, boulders, talus slopes, and other stone barriers of all conceivable sizes and shapes. 

     Additionally, the Canyon can be an extremely challenging place to hike during the spring, summer and fall because of high heat and lack of water.  In winter of course, heat is not a problem, and water (for drinking) is less of a concern.  But the difficulties and misery of physically moving through such trackless country are magnified in multiple ways by winter cold, rain, snow, ice, and mud – especially when carrying everything you need for survival on your back. 

     As it turned out, the weather conditions and rugged terrain of our routes made our trips excruciatingly difficult in many places and times, and at a couple of points (midway, both times) these conditions pushed us to the brink, to the verge of breaking.

     I mention all of this because it leads to one of the chief reasons why we did the hikes, and why the memories of those days still have powerful holds on both Jim and I.  Isn’t it true that the value of a thing that one strives for, in the end, is often proportional to the amount of effort required, risks taken, and difficulties endured to achieve it?  The “thing” here, of course, was nothing material, but rather just the experience, the accomplishment of having traveled through those magnificent and remote places under our own power. 

     The hardships we endured during these journeys were important because they drove home the aesthetic experiences.  The difficulties and risks, the inherent dangers of the place, magnified the impacts of the awesome beauty of the Canyon. The combination of intense worry, fear, physical exertion, and profound amazement at what we were seeing, hearing and feeling – gave us an acute sense of our own mortality, while at the same time creating a feeling of exaltation.

     There is no other place on the planet where I feel this mix of fear and wonder so strongly.  These are primal feelings that well up from deep within when standing on the edge of the abyss, looking down.  They are fear of and wonder about the forces beyond me, as well as my personal power over my own life.  One accidental or intentional step forward, and this life is over.  But would anything come next? 

     For me, the fear I experience at the edge of the abyss shocks me into a kind of hyper-alertness and excitement.  I can hear myself warning myself “be aware, be very aware of where you are, and what you are seeing and doing… don’t lose yourself… but yes, go ahead, look over the edge…”.  This heightened awareness focuses and stimulates the senses, and when you are in the Canyon, surrounded by its immensity and magnificence of space and rock, reality becomes a very sharp external edge that cuts through all the other internal conversations going on in your brain. 

 Perspective

     Now, 25 years later, re-reading the journals, looking at the photos, and re-tracing the routes on maps, I am struck by several things:

     The trips were remarkably symmetrical.  One went down river, the other up.  They both extended lengthwise across the western 2/3rds of Grand Canyon National Park. The first trip was below the South Rim and the second below the North Rim.  Both trips had distinctive and similar beginnings, middles, and endings.  The beginnings were fraught with worry, mishaps, and false starts.  The middles were marked by the hardest challenges and closest calls as we approached and passed the crux points:  rounding Great Thumb Mesa and reaching Mauve Saddle.   The endings both had favorable changes in weather, and despite more difficulties to overcome, we had a sense of downhill travel, that we were safely on our way home. Finally, on both trips the deep bedrock patterns of the Canyon imposed upon us our routes and passages, particularly the dichotomies of branching side canyons and the cross-cutting of faults and monoclines.

     The journals reveal our youthful exuberance, fears, self indulgence, and mysticism.  Jim was 27 during the 1978 trip and I was 22.  Now, from this vantage point in my middle age, I am a little amused, and perhaps a little chagrined about some aspects (e.g., the talk of “omens”, “pahos”, and “dope”).  Perhaps I am also a bit wistful that I have lost the physical strength, naiveté, and chutzpah to do such things now, or to think in these ways.  Twenty-five years as a scientist have changed my perspective on reality, but I can still appreciate the wonder that generated our mysticism.

     In retrospect, I also can not ignore the selfish aspects of these trips.  I exposed myself and, indirectly, my young family to risks.  Suzanne waited and worried for about 18 days during both trips, with no word of my fate until the end.  Tyson was conceived after the first trip, and he was about 14 months old when I took the second trip.  Emmy was conceived in January just before the second trip, but we did not know she was coming until I returned.  In this context and hindsight, I recognize the foolishness of the trips.  On the other hand, I was a young man, and there was something in me that needed these adventures before I did other things.

     We certainly felt that what we were doing was something very special and unique.  In retrospect, it was very special, but mostly to us.  Other people had done parts of these routes before, such as Colin Fletcher (“The Man Who walked Through Time”), and Harvey Butchart (the math professor from NAU who traveled thousands of off-trail miles in the Canyon).  We could argue that we did the routes in less time, or longer segments at once, in worse weather, or with less support (Fletcher had air drops of food and supplies!).  But, my perspective now is that all of that is irrelevant.  What matters is what the trips mean to us -- Jim and I -- not what anyone else thinks about them.  It's true though, we did want to brag about the trips when we finished them.  Maybe I still do, and these web pages are how I’m bragging now! 

     In the end, we did risk it all, and we did come close to the edge, and we did witness the most awesome natural scenery we have ever seen.  These are things that not many living people have witnessed and experienced in this manner, and in this day.  These memories and experiences about life, endurance, natural beauty, and wilderness have resonated in my memory and consciousness ever since.  Whenever I hear or read a survival story, or an account of a great wilderness adventure, such as that of Lewis and Clark and the Corps of Discovery, or Shackelton and the crew of the Endurance, I think I know what it was like, at least a little bit.  I believe I would have made it too, I would have survived; I could have done it because I endured something on a smaller scale, but similar, concentrated in two 18-day traverses in the Grand Canyon, with my brother.
