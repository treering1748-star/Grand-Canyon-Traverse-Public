           TWS

           An extraordinary day! We woke up and it was still raining -- in fact, pouring. Everything was wet - almost.  It rained so much last night that streamlets and drips of water found their way into our shelter and made us fugitives on one little dry section where we laid out our bags.

Needless to say, the situation didn’t increase our resolve to venture forth today to the Thumb and beyond. We tentatively discussed spending another rest day and waiting out the rain, but decided that walking couldn’t be much wetter, and we would be getting somewhere.

The rain let up, and so we made a break for it fairly early. The Esplanade is soaking wet! Every little wash, gully, streamlet, and arroyo has water running in it. The ground is wet in most places to the extent that just walking across you sink into mud 5-7in. deep. Walking in mud is no fun. It takes twice as much energy per mile to walk in mud than on dry, hard ground (I think).

Anyway, as we hiked on, the sun alternately smiled on us and hid from us. We were alternately drenched with pouring rain and warmed by the sun (slightly), and we were alternately, and in combination -- miserable and in awe!

The views today were nothing less than spectacular. We had many spots where the river was in view for short and long stretches. Waterfalls!  800 ft. or more high coming off the North Rim and wisping down the Redwall. In one spot we could see 3 falls coming off, and the streams they formed down these immense canyons.

Now we’re camped at the very tip of the terrace that extends toward the river from Great Thumb Point. We are exactly in the middle of our trip. We arrived here after enduring the last two miles in drenching rain and wind and crossing the first of three rough amphitheaters around the Thumb.

We decided natural shelter and fire were necessary. With a great deal of search and effort we have both and it is a nice spot. From where we sit we can see the river below in the Middle Granite Gorge and Thunder Springs and Thunder River gushing out of the side of the canyon opposite us.  It is all country I have never laid eyes on, and it is marvelous!

I write this now, after dinner sitting near the campfire -- warm. It is easy to be appreciative and to be enthusiastic in such a state. There have been many miserable and painful moments today in our effort to get here. Maybe that’s part of what a trip of this sort means. We feel good that we have come this far.

            JRS

            The Thumb. The summit.

It was still raining this morning and we got a late start, repacking and rearranging, procrastinating. But the rain was not constant, and occasionally the sun would break through on us or on some part of the canyon within our vision. We saw patches of blue among the mist and the tattered rain veils burning coldly in the wind. We agreed spontaneously that a day of effort, even in the rain and cold, was preferable to another day under a wet rock.

It was hard. And cold and painful. The mud was baby shit ankle deep. The traverses were slippery slides, a flirtation with instant death.

It was beautiful. The canyon colors velvet and deep. There were half a score waterfalls visible on the opposite wall of the Canyon, and there was the constant sound of falling water as the side canyons of our rim plunged over the Redwall. We had long views of the river, running brown as every side canyon was flowing. It was marvelous.

It was a day of misery and wonder, and hard to tell at any one moment which condition was predominant.

We made it to the Thumb in very good time, considering. We spent an hour or more exploring the terrace on Great Thumb Point, the thumbnail, looking for shelter. Found two more mescal pits, as I’d half expected to, right on the terrace. The Supai of the terraces is pocked with thousands of tinajas, all brimming.

We found a very low overhang, out near the tip. We can see the river and Tapeats Creek. Across on the canyon wall we can see Thunder River and Surprise Valley. It is a superb view, as yet seen by very few living people. Marvelous, marvelous wilderness!

Tonight, after a great deal of effort, we got a fire going and are drying our clothes. It has been worth it.
