Too much fast water in Tapeats Creek precluded walking/climbing  up through the narrows. Crazy Jug Canyon is around the corner at the far upper right.

Looking down Tapeats Creek, just below the spring, where we were able to cross -- with difficulty.  The main Tapeats Canyon is visible below.

 

JRS

Mouth of Crazy Jug.  We’re camped right on the Mauve Fault.  The route through the Redwall south of us is a classic fault ravine, nearly vertical, but it goes.  Tomas scouted it today after we got here this afternoon. 

We had another fairly difficult day.  Contouring around and above the Tapeats to Tapeats Spring was tedious and took a lot out of us, even though it was only about 4 miles.  We had to ford the stream coming from Tapeats Spring and I got wet above my waist.  It is a swift, vicious creek, this Tapeats Creek.  There’s a lot of water in this canyon.  After Tapeats Spring we stayed in the creek bed.  Another 3-4 miles of fairly easy stream bed work. 

We built camp, then did our exploring.  I walked up Saddle Canyon thinking it was a fault ravine, but could only get up about 1/2 mile.  First I was halted for a while by 100ft. waterfall that had a ramp leading to a ledge above it.  Then I was stopped for good by a puny 20ft. double falls through a slick rock chute.  We’ll go no further up Saddle Canyon.  Looks like the fault ravine is a go for tomorrow. 

The weather is atrocious. Hard cold rain nearly constant.  Waterfalls off the Redwall.  The stove don’t work and it’s almost impossible to build a fire to dry our clothes.  Other than that, everything’s just fine. 

  TWS

 All morning we worked our way around up and over Thunder River and Tapeats Creek narrows.  We were finally able to get off the Precambrian bench and drop down to creek level just below Tapeats Spring.  The water there runs fast, and there’s a lot of it.  Jim and I worked our way down but eventually had to just walk into the fast flowing current to ford, rather than risk life and limb boulder hopping with 50lb. packs. 

A couple of hard hours climbing up Tapeats Creek bed brought us here to the fault line.  Crazy Jug Canyon runs north from here above our camp.  Saddle Canyon and the ramp to the top of the Redwall are just around the bend from here.

This afternoon with camp set up Jim and I reconnoitered our route tomorrow.  The fault here breaks the Redwall into a steep ramp with vegetation growing all the way up and several bands of harder rock standing out as “climbing” sections.  The most difficult lies at the foot of the talus and 2/3 way up.  I climbed up around with some difficulty mostly due to the wet slippery rocks.  The rest of the way up will require some scrambling and pack handling with the rope.  Tomorrow the climb and Mauve Saddle. 

[A chilling incident occurred this day, not recorded in our journal entries, but recalled later by both Jim and I when we completed the trip, and were told that a plane had gone down in the Canyon on the 19th. In the late afternoon that day we heard a circling aircraft somewhere within the Canyon, not far from us.  We first heard the engine sound overhead, it faded, and then it came back again, giving us the impression  that he was circling.  The engine sound faded again and we didn't hear it any more.  It was raining and heavily overcast, and we both commented on the plane to each other, and what a bad place it was for those poor souls to be.  We didn't know it, but the plane crashed, probably seconds after we last heard it, killing 4 people. 

Jim and I went to the Grand Canyon Visitor Center after we returned to report that we were back all right, and because we had heard about the plane crash on the 19th we told them what we heard.  I recall that the crash site was somewhere just west of Powell Plateau, which means that it was near our camp that night.  I have not been able to confirm the exact spot that the plane went down, but I did find a brief NTSB aircraft crash report online.  It was a Cessna 182G, flying from Hamilton, MT to Mesa, AZ.  The crash time is listed as 1642.  The probable cause(s) were listed as "continued VFR flight into adverse weather conditions" and "spatial disorientation".  Apparently, they got lost in the clouds below the rim.

The implications of this event have haunted me a bit, ever since. There was death in the Canyon, so close to us that day.  The next day, we almost died.  What if we had seen the crash, or stumbled upon it?  What would we have done?  What could we have done?  How would it have changed the trip, and our fates? Quien sabe.]]
