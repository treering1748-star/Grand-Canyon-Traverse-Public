            TWS

             Still raining. We opened our eyes out on a misty, cloudy canyon. It had rained during the night and early morning, and it continued off and on all day, usually beginning about the time we stopped for a break or lunch. There were a couple of occasions when the clouds actually parted to reveal almost half our sky. It began raining again just as we were searching for shelter in mid-afternoon. We lucked out immediately and found this place. It’s an overhang tucked into the side of a low terrace, with a large 15-20ft. diameter mescal pit in front, and a very distinctive capped sandstone boulder to the side. We are about 1/2 miles from Great Thumb Spring. It sits up in the base of the Coconino, which is about 500ft. higher. We decided quickly when the rain began to find shelter here, rather than march up to the spring where shelter would be harder to find.

 Today we crossed the roughest, steepest section on the trip. Two large amphitheaters form on the western side of the Thumb coming over to Tahuta Point. This is the eastern limit of burro country, since they can’t get around the first and largest of these amphitheaters, which they encounter when coming from the Supai side. The first amphitheater we encountered going west is not quite as large or as steep. The second amphitheater was very large, and it took us more than an hour to traverse it. Most of the walking was actually scrambling over, up and around boulders on muddy hillsides overlooking the great drop below.  The canyon below this amphitheater is short and broad, and straight up and down.  A rock thrown off the Kiabab would go all the way off the Redwall to Bright Angel shale below.  1500ft.

 We came around Tahuta Point from the last, worst amphitheater, a distance of a couple miles, mostly on burro trail. They have indeed shown up again.  Their shit and tracks, and the conspicuous lack of low grass herald their presence. Anyway, they make darn good trails, so good I knew it couldn’t last, and it petered out as most burro trails do in a flat opening.

 Well, now we’re truly around the Thumb, technically speaking anyway. But for us the trip goes on to Supai. We hope for warmer weather, but then again we’re honored to see the Canyon when it is wet! These are the forces that made the Canyon. We hear rocks and landslides every day.

            JRS

            We made it around the last two great amphitheaters that guard the Thumb.  Past the last, the largest, we encountered burro trails and shit. The great amphitheater was, as expected, sheer, slippery, and stupendous. At the head, two huge cave-like overhangs, high above in the Kiabab, loomed over an awesome drop. The Esplanade was so reduced and steep it seemed little more than a break in that cliff that dropped through Kiabab, Coconino, Supai, Redwall, BA shale to the Tapeats. We made it through, and without incident.

From there it was little more than an hour or so’s walk to here below Great Thumb Springs. We did not go to the Spring as we are not in need of water, and it was a long walk up to the Coconino with no insurance of shelter. We instead walked down here, further into the Supai where we walked right up to a large mescal pit, near which was this overhang where we are now. There was also an Indian storage cache, now broken into.

It has rained all afternoon. The canyon is filled with mists which blow over us, clouds and clouds of rain. Torrents of water cascade off rocks and cliffs all around, and roar. The rocks glisten like silver, like snow in the moonlight, in the distances all around. Rocks are falling everywhere. I saw a house size boulder go over the Coconino, another roaring thunder of a rock fall just sounded in the darkness. The wind pries at our rock ledge, seeking us out.

We are snug and secure (relatively) our clothes are dry or drying. We have a good fire. We have adjusted, even to the wet.

We are going to make it all the way. We have, I think, met the greatest challenges we are going to. Now the only hazards are dumb shit things like stepping on your shoelaces and breaking a leg. Either one of us is prone to that.

The country ahead promises to be broader, easier and faster going. It is all downhill now and going home.

Jim's daily notes.
