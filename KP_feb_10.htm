JRS

We have begun.  We camp tonight here in Hack canyon by Willow Spring.   Our luck has held so well I scarcely dare talk about it.  The weather is superb and the air has a clarity to do it better than anything I've seen yet.  Distant images, buttes, and mountains seem close.   The fabled and deceptive magnification of desert air.

At last our umbilical is cut, but are we born or regressing into the mother’s womb?  We crawl into the cosmic cunt.

The feelings are all good.  The last contact with Deb, her voice plucking my heartstrings left me singing.  Incomparable woman.  I grieve to leave her, I long to share this with her. 

We sit beside a fire of dead fence posts, defunct rancher’s corral.  We left the good posts, the still serviceable the material where it lay.  We understand the rancher’s problem.  Use everything at least ten times before discarding.  He will not miss our firewood.

 Here is a new sensation.  We are definitely within the canyon’s precincts, yet the land’s feeling is wholly different.  The reason struck me quickly.  We have not entered the park yet.  We are on BLM land, and it’s the presence of a road. Jeep trail through it, and cow patties everywhere that shapes the gestalt of this land.  We’re still in open range.

 Willow Spring appears to lie at or near the junction of the Hermit shale and the Esplanade sandstone.  This is approximately the same formation that Jump Up Spring appears in, though appears a little lower than Jump Up.   The water is clear and the spring head is enclosed by a concrete apron and a fence of decaying planks.  It’s high enough that we can be reasonably sure the water hasn’t been contaminated by cattle.   But we’re boiling it anyway. 

The mine in Hack Canyon does not appear to have been worked recently, however extensive roadwork that must have been done no earlier than last summer leads us to believe someone may be planning to reopen it.  Surely road work as extensive as this is not done by some rancher so that he could get out and lay salt blocks.

We examined a couple of the shafts, and from the look of the ore it’s a copper mine.  However, uranium also appears in these strata, and this idea is strengthened by the fact that some of the original survey data for this quad comes from the AEC.  We feel good and we’ve got trail spaghetti cooking.  Got a couple beers in the pack.  We’re happy.
