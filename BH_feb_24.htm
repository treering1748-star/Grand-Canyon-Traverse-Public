       TWS

        Today I feel like we have finally really begun to make progress. We walked at least 12 miles; from Mount Huethawali to the terrace just east of the head of Royal Arch Canyon. I was expecting easy miles because of the trail, built by a Supai by the name of Captain Burro, from Apache Point to Bass. He was planning on building one all the way to Supai, but he didn’t quite finish. We found the trail easy enough, but it always petered out on us in crucial spots -- at the head of side canyons. Much of the day was spent in hard toil, climbing over boulders trying to find the optimal route. We continually found ourselves climbing too high on the Supai formation terraces, and having to drop down to lower ones to cross the canyons. It’s an old hiker instinct never to lose elevation unnecessarily. However, we found it best to come into the canyons low on the broad benches, if there are any, and climb up on the far side of the canyon when leaving.  It’s all route finding techniques we’re learning and will need for the miles ahead.

Both Jim and I have been amazed to hear rocks falling in the distance all day. In all my previous time in the canyon I have never heard a rock fall. But now that the cliffs in many parts of the canyon have snow, it seems this is the catalyst for such erosion. They are awesome to hear!  Usually they sound like a gunshot in the distance, with the clatter of splintered rocks landing below. While sitting around the fire tonight we heard a small slide in the head of Royal Arch.

Tomorrow we will walk to Apache. Not far, only seven miles, so we should have time in the afternoon to rest and get things done. I need to sew a small hole in the crotch of my pants -- hope it doesn’t get worse tomorrow. Very tired now and will retire.

JRS

Good, long, strenuous day today. Like to thought I’d die. Sure wish the old body would hurry up and get with it.  It is, though slowly. Much of today’s work was cross country, even though there is a trail marked on the map. Very rugged country. Terraces and benches in the Supai, steep amphitheaters, and enormous boulders on talus slopes. Traversing the bay beneath Toltec Point, we toiled through knee deep snow over ice-covered boulders. Frightening to think how easy it would be to slip, and how easily any injury out here could be fatal. I find I am still very much afraid of what we are doing. This is, by an order of magnitude, the most difficult physical challenge I’ve ever done. That comes home to me. Still we are doing it, and I’m getting into it.

I am amazed at the number of rocks we’ve heard fall this trip, and in particular this evening. Hardly a half hour goes by without a rock fall somewhere here in Aztec Amphitheater. This is no doubt due to the melting snow. It’s interesting that it is precisely the alcoves and bays that accumulate snow, and this probably contributes to making them still deeper with time. It is also interesting to note that during Colin Fletcher’s entire time in the Canyon he never wants her to rock fall.

Have seen many animal tracks -- deer, bobcat, burro mostly. And some old, nearly obliterated human tracks. So few of the latter though they did is very refreshing for a change, that condition of virgin trail being almost impossible to find in most of the Canyon. It will get still lonelier I know.

Jim's daily notes.
