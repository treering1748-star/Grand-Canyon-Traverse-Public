          JRS

          We made it down the Sinyala fault. We have cut through the dichotomies, the binary path. Like intuition that cuts across neural pathways, the drainage of the brain. Deep is the intuition of the Sinyala fault. Geologists tell us it is a structure in the Precambrian, an ancient thought of the earth. It is a fundamental of the canyon, shuffling the layers like cards above it.

We have come only 3 miles today. We could’ve gone all the way across Matkatamiba, but are in no hurry. It was boulder work, but we only had to lower the packs by rope once. When we got down to the top of the Redwall the canyon opened up into a broad Esplanade-like terrace. There are burro trails everywhere, seeking out every blade of grass.

There appears to be quite a bit of seepage out of the bottom layer of the Supai and we found a cottonwood tree just above where of the fault ravine starts cutting into the Redwall.

Walking along this terrace to head the main canyon we came 4 burros, perhaps the entire population of the canyon. I don’t see that it could support too many more. These were the first wild burros I have seen.

We are camped at the first major fork of Matkatamiba, in a cave-like hollow in the top layer of the Redwall. It drips even though it’s stopped raining outside. Beneath us the canyon flows with water into the narrow polished halls of the canyon as it goes through the Redwall. Above us to can see the Panameta Terrace and a portion of Mount Akaba.

The sun was out intermittently today and the stars are shining now.

             TWS

             Once again it was difficult to get out of the warm sleeping bag into a rainy, gray morning. To make things worse, our overhang developed a drip just over the foot of my bag, making it pretty wet. All the wood was wet, so we decided to fire up the Svea and get something warm in us, which always seems to brighten the world a little.

 About midmorning I took my pot over to the head of the side canyon to wash it in a stream there. From there I worked my way to a shelf down in the canyon on which was located one of the mescal pits we had seen the day before. Jim and I have been drooling over the possibility of finding an unopened cache or something. Maybe, who knows, a cliff dwelling!  Anyway the mescal pit was about 30ft. across, and about 20ft. high on the downhill side. A lot of work to pile all of those small rocks. Right near the center of the pile I found a complete arrowhead. Only about an inch long, and 1/2in. wide, made of a white chert. Pretty, but a little crude. Later on while looking over the large scatter of flakes in front of our overhang I found a small piece that had been worked but probably broke. It looks like this with the edges pressure flaked: 

            Then I found, almost where Jim and I laid our heads last night, a scraper or saw-like tool.  It is a flat, chert flake, about 2in. across with a couple of sides pressure flaked into serrate edges, almost like the teeth of a saw blade. Perhaps it was used in the preparations of the agaves, before or after the roasting. 

After a look into the fault ravine we decided that it indeed was the way to go. There is a fairly broad terrace, as wide as many places on the Esplanade, that provides a good route around Matkatamiba above the Redwall. We had to rope our packs down once coming down the ravine, but it wasn’t two bad. Climbing out should be work, but only for a short distance. In all it’s about five miles around Matkatamiba this way, versus 20 or so along the Esplanade. It’s well worth the climb.  

The scenery down here alone would make it worth it. The Redwall has cut down in parallel, narrow walls that snake down the canyon. Where the side side canyons enter Matkatamiba’s main bed the water has cut down below the Redwall, so that there is a 600-700ft. drop. In some places you look across to the other side only 50 to 100ft. away, yet the chasm between forces you to walk a mile upstream to turn around and come back to the spot you are looking at. 

On the way to where we camped, at the junctions of a large and small tributary in the head of Matkatamiba, we saw 4 wild burros. We surprised them coming over a ridge and got to within 50ft. or so. The closest I’ve ever gotten to them. 

We’re still hoping for sunny weather and although it did rain us today while walking, we had sun during lunch and this afternoon.
