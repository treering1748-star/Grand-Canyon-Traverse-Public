        TWS

         It was a fairly easy day today.  We came only about six miles but we accomplished our main task, that of getting around Forster canyon.  It was very steep!  The head of the canyon was reduced to talus slopes, and boulder strewn narrow benches.  It didn’t take long for us to get through the main bed, but every step for about a half hour was taken with caution and concentration.  A fall in most places wouldn’t have been too bad unless one continued to slide and went over one of the higher cliffs that the Supai forms there (20 to 50ft.).  The caution may have been partly due to what was visible below.  We could see the layers dropping off in cliffs to below the Redwall -- maybe a thousand feet down!  Sure enough, we haven’t seen any sign of burro since Forster -- they can’t get around it.

 Tonight we’re camp near the first main western side canyon of Forster.  We chose the spot mainly because there’s a trickle of water on the bottom that fills a pothole large enough to fill a canteen.  The canyons further on toward Fossil Bay seem quite dry from here so we’ll fill up tomorrow with a gallon each and head for Fossil.  It’s 12 miles from here, but it doesn’t look too bad judging by sight and contours on the topo map.

 Right near the head of our little side canyon where we camped is a mescal pit.  We have seen five of them since Bass and they all look pretty much the same.  12 to 20ft. in diameter circles of fist size rocks, piled about 4-6ft. high.  They certainly took a good deal of energy investment to build, and I assume the Indians considered the roasted agave pits worth the effort.  I’m unsure whether they just ate the pits roasted, or fermented them for mescal, or both.

 On a walk today after we set up camp we headed out toward the north end of the little terrace we’re on, and we were completely surprised and delighted with the gorgeous view of the river, the inner recesses of Forster Canyon, and terraces and buttes on the north side of the river.  It makes it all worthwhile.

 We’ve heard several more boulders and rocks fall nearby, but have been unable to actually see them come down.

 Pretty cloudy today with the sun never really showing itself from behind the light overcast.  It sure looks like we’re in for some weather. 

  JRS 

Overcast all day today, but a light one.  Good hiking weather.  My pack rides 100% better now, and the miles fairly flew by.  We only hiked during the morning and got as far as the western branch of Forster canyon.  This we figured would be the last sure water before Fossil Bay.  There is a good trickle stream here and we’ll fill up tomorrow and do the remaining 12 miles to Fossil in a day.

 We had easy hiking across Apache Terrace and then entered Forster canyon.  This canyon lived up to our expectations.  We knew it would be rough because this canyon marks the extreme western range of the burros.  It’s just too much for them.  It had me worried also as we crept around the head of the canyon high on a muddy talus slopes in the Hermit.  We did OK though, just taking our time and stopping often to study the route.  It required much concentration, and life became just one step at a time.

 At the head of this canyon, the eastern branch of the west fork of Forster, there is a mescal pit.  Damn, but it sure is something to keep coming across traces of these ancient peoples, even in such a remote and difficult place as this.

 I’m going to miss the burros.  Their trails made things easier. 

 Tom and I went exploring after we’ve we’d been here a while.  We walked down along the rim of the canyon, and as I came over a little rise of Supai sandstone I about fainted with a surprise, don’t ask me why, because there beneath us was the river!  It was an extraordinarily beautiful panorama as such things go in the canyon; the inner magnificence.  Alarcon Terrace and the lower end of Conquistador Aisle was right in front of us, Powell Plateau behind it.  In the distance to the west was the Thumb and points beyond, and just beneath us was Forster canyon, which revealed itself from this perspective to be a winding, narrow, grotto like canyon that I fairly ached with the desire to explore.  It was all one of those Canyon visions that remind you just where it is you are.

Just where it is we are.  I feel I have finally arrived.  The Grand Canyon awe has finally soaked through the shells of worry and apprehension I have been building about the miles ahead.   

Jim's daily notes.
