           TWS

           Fossil Bay at last. I’ve been intrigued with this little corner of the Canyon since I first noticed its unique drainage on the topo; and frankly, I like the name as well. There are Fossils here too.

It was an interesting walk here from this side of Forster, but of course different from anything I could have imagined. Getting out of the Forster drainage in the morning was time consuming and tedious, one little side canyon after another. But there were rewards. By the time we got even with Enfilade Point we had a gorgeous view up Conquistador Aisle – a nearly straight section of the river for 10 miles or so, and a view down the river toward the Thumb, as far as the Middle Granite Gorge. We sat there on a Supai ledge, suspended over this 800 to 1000ft. drop and rested.

After eight miles or so from last camp we were pretty much through the large side canyon work and it was a straight shot into Fossil. A fault forms the canyon and it is called the Fossil-Monument-Eremita Monocline. I like the name. The result of this fault or monocline is that all the Supai strata on this side of fossil canyon tilts down at an appreciable angle toward the bed of Fossil. So from looking at the head of Fossil from about 3-4 miles away you see this long sloping plain with exposed to red rock showing in places, making acres of paved country, and a few low ridges and rounded hills. What you don’t see are 4 long, deep, illogical washes that run across the plain into Fossil Canyon. Illogical because they are deeper at the head or start of the washes (or canyons) than at the point where they meet Fossil Canyon. Not only are they illogical, they are difficult to cross.

Just before we started on this last section I was just beginning to climb up a small ridge out of a side canyon when I spotted an arrowhead. It is made of a whitish chert rock, has a broken tip and base. It’s about an inch and a half long now. It’s been a long time since I’ve found an arrowhead. I took it as a good omen.

Also, coming across the plane to fossil I found a mule shoe! How did it get here? There are no routes that we know of from Forster to Tahuta Point, on the other side of the Thumb, which mules can traverse. Even the wild burros can’t get into this country!  It’s a mystery to me.

Now Fossil itself. It is a fine canyon. Jim and I walked all the way to just below the Coconino -- as if we didn’t have enough walking today already! There is a seep spring which enters here at the base of the Coconino, and from there we can see our whole route traversed in the past three days from Apache Point. We are camped now at the junction of the main bed of Fossil and the first side side canyon from the east. A couple of hundred yards up the main bed are dozens of potholes. Very pretty ones. The deepest -- the one Butchart describes, I guess -- is almost 3ft. deep.

We’re camped under a little overhang and tilted rock, which someone must have used for a period of time as the back wall of the rock has been filled in with a rock wall to keep the wind out. We won’t have to set the tent up tonight.

One of the best omens we’d had today has been the rain. About a half hour before we arrived it started to drip and just after we arrived it started coming down steady. We were in time to see the rocks dry, then wet. It rained for about an hour. We feel much better about the water situation on the other side now too.  It has been wet all the way from Forster with nearly every canyon having a trickle.

            JRS

            We made it to Fossil Bay. A very good day. Hard like all the others, but with a definite sense of miles covered. The Thumb is at hand. It was a bit more dissected than I thought it would be, but somewhat better per mile on the average than the terrain up to now. We also had several spectacular river views.

We found the pothole Butchart mentions and indeed it does look reliable; protected by being in the bed of the wash, about 3ft. deep and bathtub size. We also found a large cairn about 100 yds. downstream from it, beneath which was the remains of someone’s supply cache; 4 large plastic containers of trash. Also an empty Coleman can. I was somewhat angered that it hadn’t been removed, but the Coleman can will be a welcome water container, should we need one. I could figure no way this cache could have been placed here except perhaps by helicopter. Someone might have climbed down Butchart’s route, but somehow I think that unlikely.

Speaking of unlikely, Tomas discovered a mule shoe about 1/2 mile from here. How anyone managed to bully a mule down here, where even the burros don’t go, boggles the imagination.

The absence of the burro is strikingly evident in the lush growth of bunch grasses everywhere (species unknown). Burros do indeed have an impact.

Saw 2 species of plants blooming today; one specimen of loco weed and an unknown 5 petaled perennial. Will try to press a specimen and identify later.

In rained today, starting just before we arrived, for about an hour. The best possible omen! All the colors were vibrantly muted like velvet, and as we walked up Fossil Canyon, exploring to the seep spring at its head, the sun broke through the clouds just as it went over the rim.  Magnificent.

Conveniently enough, we are camped in an overhang under a large rock.  Excellent shelter and nice kitchen area.

Both of us to feel very, very good.

Jim's daily notes.
