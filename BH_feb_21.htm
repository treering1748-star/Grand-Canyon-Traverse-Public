       TWS

        Jim and I finally got on our way around 10:00 AM this morning. Our packs are heavy! We have no scales so we can only estimate the weight, but both packs must be heavier than 60lbs. It’s definitely the heaviest I’ve never carried and close to the heaviest Jim’s ever carried.

We are in high spirits as we leave and the signs are good. Clear skies, bright sun, and we see 10 deer out in Griffin tank when we get up.

It’s a little over a mile to the main road from Griffin and this section gave me better impressions of the road beyond than it deserved. The main road turned out to be fairly well traveled and so pretty muddy as well. Walking in mud with a heavy pack is a lot like walking in soft sand -- it’s hard to do. We veered off the main road whenever possible and onto the side, where the ground was usually frozen and firmer, but we had to constantly walk around the sage and fallen trees.

By lunch we had come about 8 miles or so, and it was telling on us. The road was constantly muddy and our boots were covered in it, making them feel twice as heavy. We turned north on a road at Sheep Tank, which should take is to the Park-Forest boundary road. From there we can take a jeep road which passes on the rim of Turquoise Canyon. We have camped for tonight about a mile from the boundary road. That leaves us eight or nine miles farther to go to Bass Camp. Hopefully we’ll arrive there tomorrow afternoon with enough time to make it down to the Esplanade.

JRS

            Today begins our hike.  From Griffin around the Great Thump to Hualapai Hilltop. It reminds me very much of the trip I took last year; the same frustrations about getting started, the same impossible difficulties in getting a ride to the trailhead, and the same solution in the end.  Nothing for it but to walk.

Long miles of dreary country, mud and pain in a burdened back; first day blues. So far to go to even get fairly started!  Yet, the same benefits (if any if benefits they so be, and not mere philosophical justification of necessities) are apparent by taking this course of action. We are not rendered helpless by the lack of gas powered transportation. We show by this that we are capable of conceiving and considering the age old alternative. We are independent and we’ll make our way under our own power. Once again I am connecting my points with my own solid world line and not the dashed illusion of travel by car. The world has distance!  I know for I measure it with my own body. Cars annihilate the world’s distance in precisely the only way the world has any reality for us at all, that is, subjectively. In a sense, miles traveled by car have a far more tenuous reality than those that are walked.

So here we are connecting Griffin with the Canyon.

Now for some details of our situation as I write this evening:  We have pushed ourselves hard all day and we hurt. We have forgotten the tea and have not brought enough dope. We still have not made it even so far is the rim. My crotch is already rotting. On the plus side, suffering is good for the soul. We are in good spirits. We made good time today (12 miles). The weather is good and tomorrow we should be in the Canyon!

Jim's daily notes.
