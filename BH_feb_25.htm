       TWS 

Today’s walk took us through some truly spectacular country.  Aztec Amphitheater has a large, main canyon -- that of Royal Arch Creek -- and dozens of side canyons, which all eventually drain into the Canyon of the Colorado. On the west side we encountered several side canyons with broad, open terraces of red Supai slick rock. Layered benches of reddish sandstone and fantastically carved boulders and overhangs, all perched in the sun, overlooking the abyss below. It was very pleasant walking, not only for the scenery, but because of the ease of moving over paved, level surface.

Nearly every side canyon and major tributary canyon in Aztec Amphitheater had water running, or trickling over its smooth floor. Some had especially pretty falls and water slides coursing into potholes of various sizes. In some of the prettiest country, that on the west side, we saw numerous large pot holes, open to the sun, on the broad sandstone benches. The country seemed to dry up considerably as we moved on past Point Quetzal in the afternoon. Also the difficulty of walking increased as the heads of the side canyons became steeper and rockier. Just below Apache point was the worst. The burros made things easier, since following their trails turned out to be the fastest and easiest way (usually!) of moving forward. We may miss them beyond Forester Canyon, which marks their western boundary. We’ve seen plenty of their sign – dung and tracks, but have yet to spot any.

Tonight we are camped just west of Apache Point in a side canyon. We had planned on camping on the Terrace, but it is drier than we expected and we opted for the running water in this canyon. Apache Terrace looks like some very interesting country for exploring.  It is very large, and out at the western point I believe one could view the river.

From our campsite we can see Great Thumb Mesa almost entirely now, except for the eastern side. It looks so much closer now. Hopefully we’ll be at Fossil Bay day after tomorrow, and Great Thumb Spring a couple of days later. Tomorrow it’s to Forester Canyon, and as far beyond as we can find water, probably not more than 6-7 miles from here, but Forester is rough, which is why the burros don’t get beyond it. Today we traveled 7 1/2 to 8 miles. This is of course just a figure to satisfy quantitative standards, but in no way reflects the reality with respect to how many steps taken up, down or around -- and believe me, most of it was just that!

Our physical condition seems to be improving every day, with less stiffness and soreness, and fewer blisters. Morning usually finds us energetic, and we do our best and fastest traveling then. By afternoon we’ve lost our edge, and we slow down and settle into a persistent plodding forward.  We are making progress, but it is very hard work.

JRS

Good day today. Passed through some magnificent scenery traversing Aztec Amphitheater. Travel was largely on benches in the Supai, fine limey sandstone. Many small trickle springs in the canyons (about 8) that we traversed. Also many large beautiful potholes just perched above the canyon space.

Mostly we’ve been following burro trails. They’re pretty good route finders; they know this country better than we. Just a case of ass following ass.  We’re getting pretty good at that game ourselves though; we’re not getting stranded on the benches or gaining unnecessarily altitude or having to backtrack.  Not that it was an entirely easy day though; the canyon traverses are very tedious and weary.

All of that stepping down from rocks and bumping around has been very hard on my shoulders and back. The main problem has been that my pack frame is probably too long.  I’ve not noticed it before (except on Tanner) because this is the first really heavy weight of carried with it. However, there is a way of setting the shoulder straps on this pack that adjusts it for a shorter person. This evening I changed them around and it seems to write better. Tomorrow will tell the tale. 

It was much drier around Apache Point than we’d hoped it would be. It has us worried. We figure that with 2 dry camps (not consecutive) we can get to Great Thumb Spring. Good, we have that capacity. But if it’s very dry on the Supai side, do we have enough containers to get from Great Thumb to Sinyala?  Maybe if we’re clever.

Then again, the weather pattern shaping up to the west looks promising. Snow?  I surely hope so. Even a 2 week blizzard. I’d much rather freeze than die of thirst. Everything depends on what lies ahead, and data we do not yet have. It’s out of our hands much of it ,and we’re walking the wire.

Which is the way, consciously or not, we’ve chosen for ourselves this trip. I myself at least seem to create so much more adversity than is strictly necessary. Maybe because I enjoy it getting myself out of it.

Jim's daily notes.
