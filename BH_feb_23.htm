          TWS

           Rest day today! We got up in time this morning to catch the sun rise. Breakfast, break camp, and we were on the trail. Through most of the Kiabab we waded through 1 to 1 1/2 ft. snow. Pretty rough walking, but the snow gave out to just intermittent patches through the Coconino. We traveled across Darwin Plateau around the south end of Mt. Huethawali, and are now camped on Spencer Terrace.

             What country this is! The red Supai sandstone here forms broad open pavements, breaking off into blocks near the edge. Spencer Terrace is less than 1/4 mile wide and about 2 miles long, and is sparsely covered with pinons, agave, and yucca. But most beautiful of all are the tinajas, or potholes -- which give us encouragement that our route may not be so dry after all; and they give us very tasty water for our canteens. Along the edge of the Terrace we have found dozens of them. A couple of them are over 2ft. deep and 4 or 5ft. across. It is amazing that they are here at all in this desert, but surely their existence is strictly ephemeral. This is the wettest time of the year in the canyon, and the potholes will come and go, then disappear completely in the summer heat. I am always awed by the sight of water in the desert.

Now we are camped right next to one of the prettiest tinajas. It is an unusual shape and contains perhaps 50 gallons. It is fully exposed to the sun, as are the others that we’ve seen, yet it is nearly full despite the fact that the past five days have been brilliantly sunny. When we arrived here an hour or so before noon we stripped off our clothes and just basked in the sun like a couple of lizards. It was so good after the past two days of bundled up winter camping. We can feel our energy returning.

From this spot we have been able to view our route to Fossil Bay. Through binoculars most of the Esplanade seems fairly broad with a few tight places where we’ll be on slopes. There is also snow on some north facing slopes, at least to Apache Point, and probably some on the east side of Forster Canyon. The section from Forster to Fossil looks dry, but it is far away and snow may not be visible. If we could assume that the Supai has potholes like it does here, and they have the amount of water in them that these do, then we have nothing to worry about. In any case, water is not going to be a pressing issue until perhaps beyond Great Thumb Spring on the other side.

Jim and I took a walk this afternoon farther out on the Terrace just to space out on the country a little. We dunked ourselves in one of the potholes; not feeling too guilty having done so because of the number of them. It was extremely cold water; cold enough so that we stayed in for only two dunkings each, and then hopped out to dry in the sun. It sure feels good getting that trail grime off my body in such an invigorating way.

I was surprised on the way back to camp to see two species of lizards out. I had to remind myself this is still February. Jim helped me corner them, and they turned out to be a Sceloporous undulatus, or fence lizard, and an Uta stansburiana, or desert side-blotched lizard.

This rest day was a good idea and my body is already thanking me for it. But we still have many miles to go and about two weeks to do it in. We have discussed the possibility of forgoing Royal Arch and Elves Chasm for time reasons, and we may.

JRS

Absolutely beautiful day! Easy walk down to Darwin Plateau then across beneath the western side of Mt. Huethawali to Spencer Terrace. We’re camped beside a large pot hole on the Esplanade level of the Supai. Many, many potholes. Good omen. We even found one deep enough to baptize ourselves. Cold!

It was a day of rest, of un-kinking. We’re in the Canyon at last!  Getting the old Canyon feeling. This is a very powerful spot, one of the most beautiful I’ve been to.

Did a little long distance route inspection of Aztec Amphitheater and Fossil Bay via glasses. Can’t tell much, but it does look daunting. We’ve our work cut out. All in all though, I’m becoming more confident, more positive in outlook. I know it will be difficult, but we have it in us, and conditions look very favorable as yet.

It was a long day of solitude for us, but broken all too frequently by air traffic, some of it way below the rim, and one craft right over us. Those interruptions are the rule in the Canyon these days. Yet despite that, we are perhaps, indeed likely, the only human beings in several hundred (thousand?) square miles. Wonderful!

Magnificent moon rise this evening. I was struck. And the stillness at times is tangible. Sat and watched the bats drink out of the pothole. Heard several rocks fall.

We are just beginning! There are so many days of this magnificence ahead of us, so many more wondrous miles to call this journey complete. What will we see?  How will we do?  I boggle almost off the page as I write here, thinking about it.  I must stop.
