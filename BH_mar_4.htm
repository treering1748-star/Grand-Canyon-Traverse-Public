            JRS

            It was not sunny today. In fact if anything this is the worst weather we’ve had yet. There is all the discomfort of rain and mud, with the added misery of high winds. The disappointment was tangible, and our dispositions have suffered; we’ve started snapping at each other. It looks as if the weather will continue this way for the remainder of our trip and we’re about ready to get it all over with.

The overhang we slept in last night was miserable shelter; the wind and rain just funneled in on us, and we agreed it would be pointless to spend the day there hunkered and shivering around a smoky fire. So we set off, all grim determination, for Chickapanagi Point,  9-10 miles off. We stopped only once near Hamidrik Point for lunch out of the wind in an overhang, by three large mescal pits. This crude shelter had been improved by the addition of three rock partitions. On the rock wall were painted 2 symbols, only one of which was still legible: 

We made it around the amphitheater by the same old routine; the endless going around, detours around the dichotomies of drainage.  The endless hairslipping heads of canyons.

We are so heartily sick of that, that we have decided to try bypassing the majority of Matkatamiba, that stretches out before us, and try the route down the Sinyala fault ravine. Even if this route is very difficult, without actually being impossible, it should take us no longer than going around, and it will be a welcome change of pace from the type of walking we’ve been doing.

We saw 2 wild horses this day. Supai escapees? I suspect most of the trails we’ve been following are horse trails, since they are very broad and droppings and tracks are larger than what you’d expect from burros.

There is another large mescal pit in the canyon beneath us. One wonders what these people used for fuel, since we have found it very difficult finding firewood. I suspect that these terraces once had stands of pinon and juniper that are now gone.

            TWS

            We awoke to disappointment this morning. We were hoping for sunny weather, in fact expecting it, since we saw all the signs yesterday. Men who predict the weather are often fools. We decided not to spend our rest day under a windy overhang (which brought in droplets of rain) instead we’d as soon be out on the trail walking. We got going about late morning, procrastinating as much as we could -- our bodies did not want out in that rain. We lucked out in finding good burro trails all morning.  They took us through some rough parts quickly and efficiently, but they disappeared altogether in some others.

We walked off about three miles or so, until we were near the main bed of Olo Canyon, where we crossed three large mescal pits and the remains of an overhang-rock shelter. It was a relief to get out of the rain and the driving winds, which played havoc with our ponchos. On the back wall of the shelter were two symbols:    -- maybe clan symbols?  Also, there was one curl of a bighorn. How it got there is puzzling.

From the overhang-rock shelter we walked on past Hamidrik Peak and nonstop to our campsite here on Chickapanagi Mesa. About nine miles. It was quick hiking, taking us only about 3 hours total. It was coming down pretty hard when we got here, and we set out to find a nice overhang. We walked down several side canyons and even the Sinyala fault ravine -- all Supai terrace country, and still didn’t come up with a decent place until at last, after a couple extra miles of walking, found this overhang. From here we look into a small side canyon with a large mescal pit against the far side. There is also a small one on the terrace above us. It seems we are continually camping near them, or coming by them on the trail.

Jim and I are talking now of the possibility of taking the fault ravine through Matkatamiba. Maybe.

We’re getting closer every day to Supai. We figure we could make it in two long days if we like. If the weather continues as such, we may as well.
