           TWS 

Today did not quite fit expectations. I foresaw an easy 8 or 9 miles to Supai. Well, it was easy enough the first 6 or so, then one boondoggle after another. We started down the first side canyon which comes into Havasu Canyon just above Mooney Falls in a state of excitement --- we’d be in Supai in minutes enjoying a cold soda pop. Wrong!  The canyon ended up dropping down increasingly large drop offs, one of which we roped our packs down. We came to another 50ft. drop and could see an even larger one further down, and decided we’d have to turn around. 

It was a steep, hard-earned 1/2 mile or so back up to the head of the canyon, back on the Esplanade and another mile around a point and down into Carbonate Canyon. Carbonate soon took on the characteristics of the first side canyon. Drop offs. We lowered our packs twice and labored over and down through the Supai formation to the Redwall. We started down the narrow chute through the Redwall, climbing down several falls before we found a large pool at the base of a drop off, and we’d have to swim to go farther. Back up to the top of the Redwall. Here, to our relief was a well marked trail that took us to just below Supai village about at Navaho Falls. I should’ve remembered that the village is up the canyon on top of the Redwall shelf, and the falls drop through the Redwall below.

We met several Supai on the way into the village coming from Carbonate. It must have looked pretty suspicious to see a couple of turkeys coming from that direction, instead of the usual one down the trail. They asked where we were coming from and we gave a vague reply of “from down the canyon”. They seemed slightly amazed. Our paranoia that they might be upset may be unjustified.  Anyway, we got our reservations. $9.00 apiece for two days. Expensive but I can’t blame them – I’d give us the shaft too if I were them.

It has been a long day and frustrating one. Perhaps the most frustrating and scary, but sitting here thinking about it, it was a suitable obstacle and fitting challenge to overcome in coming off the Esplanade.

I talked to Suzanne on the phone.  She’s going to try and come out and meet us at Hualapai Hilltop with Deb.  Glory!  Glory!

JRS 

A harder day than we thought. It went well enough on the Esplanade, and we made good time being as we were in top shape and with light packs. For our descent into Havasu canyon we elected to try the unnamed canyon just north of Carbonate. This we thought would save us a couple of miles. This was a mistake. We had worked our way down barely a half mile before we were utterly stopped by a sheer wall of about 50-60ft. in a light colored layer of the Supai. Much frustration. In very poor spirits, we retraced our steps then tediously worked our way up to the head of Carbonate.

 Somehow, both of us were under the impression that the last few miles to Supai were going to be a stroll. Not so. Carbonate Canyon was one was one of the hardest physical obstacles we have had to negotiate. We had to lower our packs twice to scramble down cliffs, finger and toe. We were in a very frayed condition, being tired and frustrated. This was a dangerous frame of mind, for it made us impatient and we hurried and took unnecessary chances. We were somewhat encouraged by the presence of cairn markers indicating that someone had it least pioneered a climber’s route. However when we got to the Redwall we must have missed them, because we laboriously descended this formation about 1/4 mile before being halted by a steep chute with a deep pot hole at the bottom. Back up again, cursing all the way. At the top of the Redwall we discovered the cairns we had missed, and the rest of the way was easy, on well traveled trail all the way to Supai.

 We encountered several Supais along the way and all of them asked basically the same two questions “Can you spare a joint?”  and “Which way did you come?”  Apparently there is quite a dope drought in Supai at present. We had none to spare. As for the second question, our carefully prepared lies fell apart as we spoke them; neither Tommy nor I are good liars at all. In the end, we just told part of the truth; that we came from down canyon and had been hiking many days. This last was obvious in any event. None of them seemed terribly bothered by it.

 We were asked no questions at all though in the tourist office. Our reservations were in order, and we just paid our money.

 I called the Shell station and word for Deb to pick us up on the 10th, and Tom called Suzanne and she said she’d be there at the top also. It will be a fine reunion.

 We are very tired this evening. After a meal of beans (store bought food!), bread, cheese, and coke (junk food!) we should sleep good. (The mosquitoes are very bad here.)
