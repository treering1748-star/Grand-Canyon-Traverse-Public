JRS

Thunder River!  We rose leisurely this morning and after coffee and cream of wheat we went through the food again.  A five gallon can and more were left behind -- a respectable cache still.  Our food bags are heavy now. 

It was a grunt this morning to come the 2 miles to Thunder River.  It’s everything I’ve heard and it’s a major riparian community, nearly vertical.  The green smell is overpowering.  What must it be like in summer? 

We fished for a while till the salmon eggs ran out.  Tom had the better luck, 3 to my small one, but I caught mine in Thunder River.  His were from Tapeats Creek.  Tapeats Creek by the way is a big creek, much bigger than the B.A. and it looks like a bitch to ford.  Looks like we’re going to have to head out via the benches in the Tapeats at least as far as Tapeats Spring before we venture a stream bed route.  This is all pretty much as expected. 

We are camped in a beautiful spot about 300ft. above Tapeats Creek and beside Thunder River.  Cottonwoods grow around us and there is a splendid Redwall face across from us.  The sky has been constantly changing today.  The clouds fly by very fast, when flash!  A heartbreakingly long piece of blue sky flows overhead, the sun hits you bright, and the temperature rises 5°.  Then gone!  And a light shower of rain, fairly warm rain, and a curtain of green humidity envelops you.  It reminds me of nothing so much as July and August afternoons in the Pecos, strangely enough. 

Did some extensive trail cobbling on my right boot whose sole is threatening to go to hell.  Tried SCA Eric’s idea of stitches imbedded in knife cuts in the sole.  Looks promising.  I also put in 2 baling wire stitches.  I pray it all holds together. 

My knee bothering me a bit now, but I’m resting it and I think it’ll be all right if I watch myself.  Will wrap it again tomorrow. 

It looks like 7-9 miles tomorrow of fair difficulty.  The beauty of this spot has made our morale rise considerably. 

 TWS

Our food stores replenished we saddled up this morning, and stooped over with the weight, we plodded the 2 miles to where the trail out of Surprise Valley drops into this gorge.  Thunder River.  Birth of a river from rock.  The water cascades first over a wide slide then shoots down in a series of falls, the highest over 100ft.  Greenery everywhere, despite the winter season.  The Precambrian rock tilted forms the lower half of the canyon, with all the Redwall diminished none, forming the upper.  Thunder River pours down from beneath the Redwall into the Tapeats Creek narrows and joins the sizable river there already.  It is a powerful tributary river moving fast down through a rocky stream bed. 

One look and a little exploration upriver and it became obvious our route would have to be above on top of the Precambrian rock, on a narrow talus sloping bench formed between upper Paleozoic rocks and the Precambrian below.  It’ll be tedious work tomorrow in our effort to make upper Tapeats and the junction with Saddle Canyon, but the views into the creek bottom and up toward Mauve should be interesting.

Tonight will be our 5th straight in this Thunder River country.  It seems that since we got to Deer Creek uppermost on our minds has been Mauve Saddle.  It’s definitely a tough goal.  Now there’s bound to be snow up there, and from all accounts the route up is not easy to find or follow.  Three days are allotted on our permit for the traverse and ascent up to the saddle.  Tomorrow hopefully we’ll be looking up and down the fault line.  The Crazy Jug monocline and Mauve Fault will provide us the route through this otherwise impassable country, and we shall follow it.
