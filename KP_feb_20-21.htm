TWS

 I write now from the warmth and security of Mauve Cabin.  Yesterday, the 20th, was our hardest day yet.  We climbed up Saddle Canyon the mile or so separating our camp of the night before and the full ramp leading up to the Redwall.  We climbed our way up the ramp mostly on hands and knees using the thick brush as handholds up the 100% + slope.  It had rained all night and sporadic rainfall would continue to come down on us all day.  Our clothing was soaked by the time we reached the climbing section of the ramp, and it was most cold and trying of patience as we managed the rope to haul up the packs.  My pack was especially difficult, seeming to hang up on every rock protrubence and bit of brush.  Once up and over the difficult section we had to claw our way up the rest of the ramp to the top.  We were then about 1000ft. above our last night’s camp, but another 600ft. or so of Supai sandstone was yet to be climbed before we could reach the Esplanade and our traverse route. 

The next hour we climbed mostly with our packs on, traversing between Supai ledges until we could find the cracks we could climb up.  Finally we made the last ledge before the top, and a particularly hard climb forced us to drop packs and haul them up with rope.  On top of the Esplanade we looked straight across to Mauve -- it appeared to be so close.  We found an overhang and with much difficulty got a fire going.  We needed something warm to drink badly.  The rain had turned to snow, and wind had picked up.

After a can of tuna and a cup of hot chocolate we set out again to work our way around to the saddle.  The Esplanade in this section was covered heavily with pinon, juniper and brush.  Down on the slick rock, paved ledges we slogged through sections of ankle-deep mud.  In one spot we found numerous flakes, and a few steps further and both Jim and I each found a point. 

About two miles from the Saddle we encountered thick brush -- manzanita and bear berry, and we literally had to fight our way forward for every step.  For the most part we were contouring on steep slopes.  Mud and snow continually would slide out beneath us.  Finally we made it around the corner, but we weren’t there yet.  The actual ridge line of the saddle was still up at the very head of Saddle Canyon.  We couldn’t see how far it was snowing, and we were fairly well socked in.

At this point we were very tired.  It was late afternoon, we had climbed over 2000ft. and 7 long miles of slope and brush in the rain, but our spirits were high -- we must be so close to the cabin. 

We set out up the canyon and soon came into deep snow and thicker brush.  We struggled along through drifts above the knees weaving in and out of the thickets.  Each ridge coming down from the canyon side seemed to be the one, and then topping over it we’d see yet another up ahead, a little higher.  We continued till both of us realized that exhaustion was near, and our situation was beginning to look serious.  We knew we were close to the cabin, but headway through the snow and brush was getting harder and harder.  We were both soaked to the bone.  We hadn’t had a significant meal all day and energy reserves were very low.  We were starting to shiver.  I suggested we drop packs to head up to find the cabin, get warm and return.

Our packs off we were able to move much quicker up the draw, and about 1/3 of a mile further up we spotted the cabin.  Frankly -- I was disappointed -- the stove is an old handmade job that smokes and has been mistreated into almost 0 efficiency (if it ever was), and the food supplies that the back country ranger mentioned turned out to be assorted reject food left here by backpackers for the last 7 years.  Well, we didn’t get warm or eat, but now we were sure the cabin was here.  From sunlight off the Coconino way down canyon we could see that sunset wasn’t far off.  We trudged down to the packs, grabbed our bags and clothes, and got back to the cabin just at dark.  It was a close call.  If the cabin had been much further, our survival -- outside with wet clothes and our state of exhaustion -- would’ve been very doubtful.

Today (the 21st ) looks much better.  Our clothes dry (slowly) over the stove.  We’ve eaten hearty and Jim even produced 13 tortillas from the cached flour.  The cabin is warm even though it’s something of a blizzard outside. 

Our stay in here and Mauve Cabin has been very productive.  Clothes, backpacks and gear all dried.  Packs and clothes mended.  Good food and lots of hot drink have raised our spirits and energy levels.  We have made it, and we still have our determination.  The Canyon has tested our resolve and spirit again.

A short reconnoiter this afternoon showed the trail leading from in back of the cabin to the break in the Coconino.  We can descend here whatever the weather.  Tomorrow begins a new effort to reach and get over Flint Saddle, and to see all the country between here and there. 

JRS

Mauve Saddle.  It was brutal work getting here.  It nearly finished us.  When we got here I was soaked thoroughly and shivering, even after exertion.  Night was coming and the weather was very bad.  Knee deep snow, near impenetrable brush and a saddle that seemed to keep receding from us.  Many times I was sure we were nearly there only to find the saddle was still further.  And the brush! Oak thickets, manzanita and locust -- a jungle.  And here we are, slogging through snow on top of this shit, after a long hard day of hiking. 

To start the morning we climbed through the Redwall, a couple of grade 5 pitches, and scrambled up the ravine about 800ft.  Very difficult hauling the packs up after us.  Then another 500ft. or so in the Supai, more scrambling and grade 5 climbing.  By this time we were already soaked, and so with considerable trouble (and precious gas) we got a fire started under an overhang.  An hours rest and we were off again, mud slogging and underbrush fighting.  Damned hard hiking, damned hard.  It sure took the wind out of us.  We were so down in exhaustion and depression, we bitched and snapped at each other all day. By the time we got here we were on the thin line.  I, at least, was fighting for survival.  After we abandoned our packs I was running on emergency reserve.  If the cabin was a half mile further, or it had taken us another half hour to find it, I might not have been able to continue.  I couldn’t stop moving, even for a few minutes, I was so cold I might not have been able to start again. 

There was little jubilation when we reached the cabin.  We were too depleted, and all we could do was start a fire, warm up for a few minutes, then head back the 1/3 mile or so to our packs for sleeping bags and dry clothes.  With a broken trail and light load, we managed handily, but even so it was rapidly getting dark when we got back.  We spent a crude primitive evening huddled over this barely functional stove and griped at each other over a guttering, improvised candle.  This the highest point in elevation we will reach; it was a severe low point for our morale. 

Today (21st ) we have got it back together again. Our clothes are dry, or nearly so, the cabin is warm, and we’ve been pigging out.  Gathered firewood, melted snow, and guzzled hot drinks all day.  Made a baker’s dozen tortillas.  Patched a hole I burnt in my pants, and improvised a fine pair of gaiters from an old nylon tarp I found in the attic.  This cabin, primitive though it is, saved our lives.  We will be able to head out tomorrow replenished with dry clothes on our bodies and in our packs.  It’ll be hard no doubt since the trail is overgrown, and nothing about this hike has been easy.  The weather will probably remain terrible.  As I write this we’re socked in solid, it’s snowing, and there’s a howling wind.  Great conditions.  But we’ll be going downhill tomorrow, dropping quickly (I hope) below the snow belt to what we’re more used to, and better able to cope with -- cold rain.  I really believe the worst is behind us, mainly because it’s difficult to imagine much worse conditions ahead.
