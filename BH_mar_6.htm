  Bass to Havasupai, February 20 to March 10, 1978

            TWS 

I got up the morning feeling pretty negative. It rained again last night an the skies were gray and threatening. The limestone overhang we stayed under dripped continually so we had to set the tent up inside, with only Jim’s poncho. It didn’t work that great and my sleeping bag ended up getting pretty wet. This is the tenth day of rain and it does get tiring.

 We got going, and by the time we reached the fault ravine on the west side the sun was out and our spirits were up. It was work coming up, as the bed of the ravine was littered with gigantic boulders, and in places there were waterfalls and cascades.  We got around all of these easy enough on the talus slopes to the right or left. The last few steps of the climb rewarded us with a magnificent view of Mount Sinyala, straight ahead and now only a couple of miles distant across Sinyala Canyon. A fault ravine cuts down again here into Sinyala Canyon and emerges in a line on the other side.

 Fine country. The saddle between the west fault ravine of Matkatamiba and the east side of Sinyala Canyon is where we are camped. There’s a couple of mescal pits here and lots of nice overhangs, which the Indians obviously used. Within an hour or so of looking we found a half dozen arrowheads and several scrapers. Some of the points are very tiny and delicate. Jim found one that’s only about 1/4in. long.

 We went out to a point on Matkatamiba Mesa overlooking Sinyala Canyon and watched the sunset.

JRS

We’ve made it out of Matkatamiba today via the Sinyala fault. It was steep and rough -- but short, only a mile or so. Where the fault canyon started into the Redwall we found a mescal pit, and at the top in the saddle between Sinyala and Matkatamiba Canyons we found a larger one, plus several shelter sites, one of them tagged with a number: AZ.;B;10;90 May 13,76. The sites were very rich in chippings and shards, old corncobs, and points. I found a very nice thumb scraper and two perfect, tiny points no bigger than my little finger. We both caught the arrowhead bug and spent hours this afternoon with our noses to the ground.

The weather was wet this morning and last night, and we both had wet sleeping bags despite tent and cave. The clouds cleared up largely by this afternoon and we were able to dry our possessions, and, glory!  Bathe! It was a renewal.

It is good to be back on the Esplanade. Mt. Sinyala looms to the west, a very impressive sight, and the Sinyala fault almost seems to point to it. This is a focus of a sort, this place. I feel it from the logic of the landscape, the way it lies; I feel it from the Indian site and the mescal pit, their placement here in the saddle; I feel it in the weather which, at last, is beginning to change for real.

The mescal pit. How often this trip we’ve camped and camped in the best places, beside a mescal pit. The former inhabitants of this country, this canyon, have been with us constantly.

I have an impression of Matkatamiba I haven’t recorded yet. We walked down the canyon as it deepened and narrowed, burrowing into the red wall.  That rock polished white and gray blue was just beginning to be stained its Redwall color and streamers as the Supai bled down onto it.

I think we were spotted by a plane today, here on the saddle. The sum bitch flew near over us eastward on his trip out (it was a turkey tour) are then on the way back he came zooming over a ridge directly over our heads and very low, as if he wanted to catch us by surprise. Asshole.
