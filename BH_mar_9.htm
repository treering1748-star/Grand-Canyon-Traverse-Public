  Bass to Havasupai, February 20 to March 10, 1978

            TWS

           Lazy day today. We got up late and the skies were gray, but soon it cleared up and we’ve had sun with high altitude clouds all day. We took a walk down to Mooney falls and as far as the first river crossing below.  Havasu Creek is in flood stage right now it is a chocolate brown instead of its usual turquoise blue. It’s also about three times as big as it normally as, or at least how I remember it. I guess it was about four years ago that I was last here.

It has been sort of anti climactic arriving here. Today we just laid around in the sun, dreaming about the reunion with the ladies, showers, clean clothes, and beer. We are ready to come out and start up our lives in that other world. The realization that we have done what we’ve done has not fully crystallized.

Tomorrow -- on up to the top. It’s a little over ten miles, but with our packs empty or nearly so, we should clip off the miles quickly. We planned our food well. We have enough oatmeal for couple more meals, and enough rice for the same. We have a few other odds and ends of things, enough to last a couple of days if necessary, but all in all it was pretty close to accurate planning. We’ve purchased a few “luxuries” here at the Supai trading store -- sardines and soda pop and an ice cream cone. Ahhh, the comforts of civilization.

JRS 

A day at Supai. We went down below Mooney Falls and poked around a bit. Looked up a side canyon that went back about a quarter mile and fetched up against a 100ft. chute in the Redwall. Spectacular.  The forces of water; how they carved channels like these. There were numerous travertine pools and of deep, clear water.  There were ferns growing green against the Redwall. It was an enchanted place.

 The water here is not the pale blue I had come to expect from pictures since the river is in flood stage and is a muddy torrent. Still the effect of the travertine formations and the beauty of the falls was not exaggerated. It is a paradise.

 We toyed with the idea of climbing the scaffold to the Bridal Veil mind, but in the end did not. We are both kind of burned out by now, and don’t feel like doing much of anything really. To tell the truth were a little bored.  Spent much of the afternoon sleeping in the sun.

 Two Supai came down to visit us, hoping for a joint, but we had none to give them. What Supai needs is a good reliable dealer. We talked with them, though, for a long time and as it turned out we had many mutual acquaintances. One of them named Don knew Deb and most of the Tusayan crowd, and of course Eric. We’ve met a lot of Supai down here and most of them seem to know him. They asked us how we came down, and rather than lie, we just told them the truth. It didn’t seem to faze them a bit. I had been led to believe that it would be much more touchy subject than has turned out. Perhaps the average Supai doesn’t really give a shit.  Or perhaps the ones we’ve talked to were just ignorant of the situation.  I don’t know. Anyway, it looks as if we’ve gotten away with it, and with a minimal amount of lying. Perhaps we’ve been overly paranoid all long.  Perhaps we’ve just been lucky.

 So much for Supai. I am just too saturated with canyon sweat right now, and I’m ready for trail’s end. The next time I come down here will be able to give it more attention.  It is a beautiful place.

 Tomorrow: up and out.
