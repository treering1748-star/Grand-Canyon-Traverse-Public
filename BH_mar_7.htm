           JRS

           It has been absolutely clear all day. A sunny day at last. We have made it a rest day, the last day we spend alone, for tomorrow we make it to Supai. 

We spent the day hunting arrowheads and exploring part of Matkatamiba Mesa. I found two more points, one of them perfect, and a rusty old tin cup that still holds water. Right in the fault saddle there are three cairns, one of which was has a chain and hook and an old iron ring. Prospectors perhaps. We also found two more numbered and labeled Indian sites:  AZ.; B; 10; 91 and AZ.; B; 10; 93. We couldn’t find 10; 92.

 We’ve been buzzed a couple more times today. Now it’s sets my teeth on edge every time I hear a plane.

 It has not been too noteworthy a day other than that. We have soaked up a lot of long missed sunshine, and savored the end of our solitude. It is almost over. Supai will be an anti climax. I don’t mind. The sunshine here at the end has sealed it for me nicely.  I want to go home.  I want to drink beer and eat a steak.  I want to hold Deb.

TWS 

Finally a sunny day. Today we decided to take just for ourselves. To lay in the sun like a couple of lizards. Bags, shirts, pants, socks everything was laid out on the rocks to dry. There’s not a cloud in the sky.

 We started wandering about around mid-morning – poking through the scatter of flakes around the mescal pit and overhang. I found a tiny white point in the style of the small ones we found yesterday; it’s missing the very point, and one side of the base. Jim found a couple more points as well -- one of them is complete.

 Our success of finding so many artifacts we decide must be due to the remoteness of this site, that it hasn’t been picked over, and that we’re here at the right time, just during the spring rains, and they’ve been neatly, cleanly exposed. We find most of them in the drainages, the chert rock standing out against all the other sandstone rocks and pebbles.

 The richest spot where we found six or so points is that overhang-boulder shelter that is situated near a seep emanating beneath a Supai terrace. The seep looks as if it may be fairly reliable, at least during the wet season, and there are some ferns growing up in it. The seep may be the reason for the heavy concentration of lithics near it, being that the shelter was the most often used when people were in the area.

 In addition to the arrowhead as we found many crude and a few fine scraping or cutting tools. We suppose they may have been used in the processing of the agaves.

 We went for a walk around noon out on Matkatamiba Mesa and found two more mescal pits in the nearest drainage on the west side. Lots of flakes around and we found a few point fragments. We also went out to the large side canyon on the eastside, where we could almost see the river. From there we could see the shelf above the Redwall, in the side canyon and above the Colorado, where it is quite broad and would be fairly easy walking.

 Tomorrow Supai and people. It’s been two weeks since we’ve seen another living soul or vestige of man (except the god damn airplanes).  We’re hesitant to leave this behind, but anxious too for the comforts and the people we love. We’re looking forward to sitting back with a cold beer and letting the awareness sink in that we actually did it.
